\section{Discontinuous Learning and Sudden Insight}
\label{sec:sudden_insight}

Traditional analyses of spatial learning often average performance metrics across a group of animals, typically yielding a smooth, exponential learning curve. However, as demonstrated by \cite{gallistel2004learning}, averaging over individual learning trajectories can mask the true, often discontinuous nature of cognitive acquisition. When analysing the cumulative direct paths to the water port on an individual basis, we observe that spatial learning in mice is highly non-linear.

Approximately half of rewarded mice exhibited ``sudden insight'', which matches observations made by Rosenberg et al \cite{rosenberg2021mice}. Sudden insight is a behavioural shift characterised by an abrupt, persistent increase in the gradient of cumulative direct paths to the target.

To formalise the detection of this cognitive shift, we evaluated three change-point methodologies. First, following Rosenberg et al. \cite{rosenberg2021mice}, we modelled the rate of direct paths to the target as an inhomogeneous Poisson point process, fitting a 4-parameter sigmoid function [SIG] to identify the time of half-maximal rate change.

Secondly, we used a Bayesian Change Point [BCP] Detection algorithm to identify an optimal discrete point of behavioural separation, $\tau$. We operated under the assumption that the path lengths before and after the insight event are drawn from two distinct Poisson distributions, defined by rate parameters $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$. The posterior probability of the change point, given the sequence of path lengths $X$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    P(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau | X) \propto P(X | \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau) P(\lambda_1) P(\lambda_2) P(\tau)
\end{equation}

where the likelihood function $P(X | \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau)$ assumes $x_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ for $t \leq \tau$ and $x_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ for $t > \tau$. 

For the third approach, we implemented a third method: a threshold-based detection of the "elbow" in the cumulative direct path curve [CDPE]. By evaluating a rolling average of the direct path rate, this method reliably isolates the onset of sustained exploitation without being penalised by interspersed exploratory bouts.

While this BCP approach significantly improved detection compared to the sigmoid model, it proved highly sensitive to intermittent exploratory paths. Because mice frequently alternate between target exploitation and free exploration, these intermittent long paths mathematically disrupt the post-insight Poisson distribution, causing the Bayesian detector to artificially delay the change point.

Reuslts are shown in Figure \ref{fig:sudden_insight}, where we plot a detailed analysis of the three detected change points along with supporting data, and include a summary across all mice which shows the time of sudden insight from the three methods against the occurance of rewards and direct paths to the water port. 

We observe that none of the three methods is robust. That SIG struggles when insight occurs early, and that the BCP approach is most sensitive to the presence of intermittent exploratory bouts, the CDPE approach appears to be the most consistent. However, the correlation of the three detectors appears to be a reasonable indicator of the existence of sudden insight. Where learning is more gradual, the BCP tends to predict early and the SIG tends to predict late, which CDPE tends to be somewhere between. The divergence of these detectors, combined with a distance or rate threshold, threshold may provide a robust detector for sudden insight.

While the emergence of direct paths with high frequency provides a clear marker for discontinuous learning, we also observe a gradual decrease in the length of \textit{non-direct} paths leading to the target. This divergence indicates a crucial cognitive distinction between learning the absolute spatial location of the water port (``knowing where'') and optimising the navigational heuristic required to reach it efficiently from home (``knowing how''). This latent optimisation strongly aligns with the development of an internal Cognitive Map \cite{tolman1948cognitive}, which is constructed during unstructured exploration prior to the onset of sudden insight.
