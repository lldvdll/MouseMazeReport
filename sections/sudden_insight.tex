\section{Discontinuous Learning and Sudden Insight}
\label{sec:sudden_insight}

Traditional analyses of spatial learning often average performance metrics across a cohort of animals, typically yielding a smooth, exponential learning curve. However, as demonstrated by \cite{gallistel2004learning}, averaging over individual learning trajectories can mask the true, often discontinuous nature of cognitive acquisition. When analysing the cumulative direct paths to the water port on an individual basis, we observe that spatial learning in mice is highly non-linear.

Specifically, $5$ out of the $10$ rewarded mice exhibited ``sudden insight''---a behavioural shift characterised by an abrupt, persistent increase in the gradient of cumulative direct paths to the target. To formalise the detection of this cognitive shift, we analysed the trajectory efficiency (measured in total steps per bout) from the home node to the water port. 

We deployed a Bayesian Change Point Detection algorithm to identify the optimal point of behavioural separation, $\tau$. We operated under the assumption that the path lengths before and after the insight event are drawn from two distinct Poisson distributions, defined by rate parameters $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$. The posterior probability of the change point, given the sequence of path lengths $X$, is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    P(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau | X) \propto P(X | \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau) P(\lambda_1) P(\lambda_2) P(\tau)
\end{equation}

where the likelihood function $P(X | \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \tau)$ assumes a Poisson process, such that $x_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ for $t \leq \tau$ and $x_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ for $t > \tau$. 

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:sudden_insight}, the algorithm successfully isolates the pre- and post-insight distributions. Interestingly, we note that significant reward accumulation frequently occurs prior to the detected change point ($\tau$). This suggests a period of latent spatial exploration occurs before the mouse confidently optimises its exploitation route. 

Furthermore, while the emergence of direct paths provides a clear marker for discontinuous learning, we concurrently observe a gradual decrease in the length of \textit{non-direct} paths leading to the target. This divergence indicates a crucial cognitive distinction between learning the absolute spatial location of the water port (``knowing where'') and optimising the navigational heuristic required to reach it efficiently from home (``knowing how''). This latent optimisation strongly aligns with the development of an internal Cognitive Map \cite{tolman1948cognitive}, which is constructed during unstructured exploration prior to the onset of sudden insight.
