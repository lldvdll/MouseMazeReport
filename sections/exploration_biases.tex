\section{The Universality of Exploratory Heuristics}
\label{sec:exploration_biases}

\subsection{Modelling Geometric Biases}
While unstructured exploration appears random, Rosenberg et al. \cite{rosenberg2021mice} demonstrated that mouse navigation in a labyrinth is heavily governed by geometric heuristics. Rather than memorising exact spatial routes, mice employ a spatially-invariant, second-order Markov model (a ``biased random walk'') at every T-junction. 

This model collapses all maze decisions into four fundamental turning biases based on the mouse's approach vector:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stem-Forward ($P_{SF}$):} The probability of continuing forward into the maze when arriving from a parent stem (avoiding a U-turn).
    \item \textbf{Stem-Alternating ($P_{SA}$):} The probability of alternating turn directions (e.g., Left then Right) when moving forward through consecutive stems.
    \item \textbf{Branch-Forward ($P_{BF}$):} The probability of continuing forward when arriving from a child branch (avoiding a U-turn).
    \item \textbf{Branch-to-Stem ($P_{BS}$):} The probability of selecting the parent stem (heading toward the exit) rather than the adjacent child branch when moving forward.
\end{itemize}

To validate the presence of these geometric rules, we extracted the transition probabilities for all mice across the different behavioural modes. Figure \ref{fig:turning_biases} successfully reproduces the baseline heuristic behaviour: mice exhibit a strong preference for forward momentum ($P_{SF}, P_{BF} > 0.5$) and alternating turns ($P_{SA} > 0.5$) during unstructured exploration. Crucially, these heuristics diverge sharply during reward-seeking (\textit{drink}) and exiting (\textit{leave}) behaviours, confirming that the \textit{explore} mode is governed by a distinct, geometry-driven random walk.

To quantify how strictly a mouse adheres to these biases, we calculate the Intrinsic Entropy (Cross-Entropy, $H$) of its trajectory. For a sequence of node transitions $X$ of length $N$, evaluated under the learned bias probabilities $P$, the cross-entropy per decision is:
\begin{equation}
    H = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^{N} \log_2 P(x_{t+1} | x_t, x_{t-1})
\end{equation}
A perfectly random walk at a T-junction yields an entropy of $\log_2(3) \approx 1.58$ bits per step. Lower values indicate highly structured, predictable movement.

\subsection{Innate Rules vs. Cognitive Anchoring}
We trained the 4-Bias model on strictly unstructured sequences (Mode 2: \textit{Explore}) using rigorous 5-fold cross-validation. Figure \ref{fig:generalization_anchoring}A displays the out-of-sample cross-entropy distributions. Strikingly, models trained on peer mice generalized just as well as models trained on the subject itself, with the variance remaining uniform across all evaluations. 

Furthermore, an independent Welch's t-test revealed no significant difference in the baseline exploratory entropy between the rewarded ($\mu = 1.521$ bits) and unrewarded ($\mu = 1.527$ bits) cohorts ($p = 0.65$). This proves that the 4-Bias geometric heuristics are an \textit{innate} biological baseline, universally employed by all mice regardless of reward conditioning.

However, while the baseline rules are universal, the \textit{strictness} with which they are applied varies significantly based on an animal's cognitive map. To evaluate this, we plotted a mouse's overall fixation on the target (defined as the proportion of all leaf visits made to Node 116) against its intrinsic entropy during strictly free-roaming \textit{explore} bouts (Figure \ref{fig:generalization_anchoring}B).

We observe a significant negative correlation ($r = -0.56$). This reveals a phenomenon of ``cognitive spillover.'' When a mouse possesses a strongly anchored spatial goal (high target fixation), its mental map structurally constrains its behaviour even when it is freely exploring. The presence of the target forces the mouse to abandon random wandering (approaching 1.58 bits) in favour of highly efficient, rigid geometric heuristics.
